What is a detail?

A wash cleans the surface. A detail fixes what's underneath.

Most people use "wash" and "detail" as the same word. They're not. A wash removes loose dirt. A detail removes what a wash leaves behind, corrects the paint, and protects it. Here's the actual difference, what skipping it costs you over time, and how to tell when your car is overdue.

What detailing actually means

A car wash removes loose surface dirt. It doesn't touch bonded contaminants. The brake dust, industrial fallout, tree sap, and road tar embedded in the clear coat. It doesn't correct paint. It doesn't protect paint. When you drive away from a tunnel wash, the surface is clean but the paint underneath is still carrying everything it was carrying before.

A detail starts where a wash stops. Here's what actually happens in a full detail:

  • Decontamination. A clay bar or iron remover pulls bonded particles out of the paint so the surface is chemically clean, not just visually clean.
  • Correction. Machine polish removes swirl marks and light scratches by leveling the clear coat. This is what makes paint look like glass again.
  • Protection. Wax or ceramic seals the corrected surface against UV, water spots, bird drop etching, and road grime for weeks to months.
  • Interior. A proper interior detail vacuums, brushes, wipes, and steams. It removes embedded dirt, pet hair, and odors that surface wipe-downs miss.

What happens if you skip it

Paint damage is cumulative. Every season you skip a detail, the clear coat gets a little thinner from UV exposure and embedded contaminants. Swirl marks from tunnel brushes deepen. Water spots etch into the surface. Plastic trim fades and chalks. Once the clear coat is worn through, the damage is permanent. Paint correction can't fix what's gone.

Neglected interiors compound the same way. Pet hair and grit work into fabric fibers. Stains set deeper over time. The longer you wait, the more a standard clean can't reach, which is when a job becomes a Deep clean (quoted) instead of a standard interior detail.

One detail a year, done properly, costs far less than paint correction on a car that's been ignored for three years.

Signs your car needs to be detailed

Any one of these is a signal. Two or more means it's overdue.

Paint looks dull or hazy

The paint used to look deep and glossy. Now it looks flat. This is oxidation. UV and contaminants breaking down the clear coat. Correction and a protective layer fix it.

No mirror-like reflection

On a freshly detailed car, you can read the reflection in the paint. If you can't see a clear image, the surface has micro-scratches and contaminants diffusing the light.

Swirl marks in direct sunlight

The circular marks you see when the sun hits the hood at an angle. These are from automated wash brushes, dirty cloths, or wiping dry paint. Machine polish levels them out.

Water spots that won't wash off

Water spots that stay after a wash are etched into the clear coat. A quick spray wax won't fix them. You need decontamination and correction.

Paint feels rough after washing

Run a clean hand over a just-washed panel. It should feel like glass. If it feels like fine sandpaper, there are bonded contaminants in the surface. A clay bar removes them.

Faded or chalky plastic trim

Faded black or gray plastic trim looks gray and chalky instead of deep and satin. Chemical Guys VRP restores the color and protects it. This is included in the Premium detail and available as an add-on on Essential.

Foggy or yellowed headlights

Yellowed headlights throw less light and look rough. Sand, polish, and a UV seal bring them back to clear. This is a $35 add-on on any package.

Interior odors, set-in stains, or embedded pet hair

A vacuum gets the loose stuff. Set-in odors, stains, and pet hair woven into the fabric need a proper interior clean, and sometimes a steam clean or deep clean to fully remove.
